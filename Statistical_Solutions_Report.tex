\subsection{Problem 4.33: Comparing Estimators for the Normal Distribution}
\textbf{Solution:}
We used R to simulate 100,000 samples, each of size $n=100$, from the Standard Normal Distribution $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. For each sample, we computed both the sample mean and the sample median.

The mean of the true distribution is 0, and the median is also 0. An estimator is considered "better" if it exhibits a lower Mean Squared Error (MSE), which represents the expected value of the squared difference between the estimator and the true parameter.
$$MSE(\hat{\theta}) = E[(\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2] = \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^2 + \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})$$
Since both estimators are unbiased for $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, the MSE is exactly their variance.

The analytical variances are:
- Variance of the Sample Mean: $\sigma^2 / n = 1 / 100 = 0.01$
- Asymptotic Variance of the Sample Median: $\approx \frac{\pi}{2} \cdot \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \approx 1.57 \times 0.01 = 0.0157$

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Estimation_prob1_plot.pdf}
    \caption{Sampling Distributions of the Sample Mean and Sample Median for $N(0,1)$}
\end{figure}

The simulated MSE values computed from R are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MSE of Sample Mean:} $\approx 0.01004$
    \item \textbf{MSE of Sample Median:} $\approx 0.01557$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Conclusion:} The \textbf{sample mean} is the better estimator for the center of a normal distribution because it has a lower Mean Squared Error (lower variance). The sample mean utilizes all the information in the sample efficiently, whereas the median relies mostly on the middle values, leading to higher variability.

\subsection{Problem 4.36: Bias-Variance Tradeoff for Variance Estimators}
\textbf{Proof:}
We know that $\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$.
For a Chi-squared distribution with $k$ degrees of freedom, the mean is $k$ and the variance is $2k$. Therefore:
$$E\left[\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}\right] = n-1 \implies E[S^2] = \sigma^2$$
$$Var\left(\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}\right) = 2(n-1) \implies \frac{(n-1)^2}{\sigma^4} Var(S^2) = 2(n-1) \implies Var(S^2) = \frac{2\sigma^4}{n-1}$$
The MSE of the unbiased estimator $S^2$ is purely its variance:
$$MSE(S^2) = Var(S^2) + \text{Bias}^2(S^2) = \frac{2\sigma^4}{n-1} + 0 = \frac{2\sigma^4}{n-1}$$

Now let us consider the biased estimator $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{n-1}{n} S^2$.
Its expected value is:
$$E[\hat{\sigma}^2] = \frac{n-1}{n} E[S^2] = \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2$$
Thus, its bias is:
$$\text{Bias}(\hat{\sigma}^2) = \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2 - \sigma^2 = -\frac{1}{n}\sigma^2$$
The variance of $\hat{\sigma}^2$ is:
$$Var(\hat{\sigma}^2) = \left(\frac{n-1}{n}\right)^2 Var(S^2) = \frac{(n-1)^2}{n^2} \frac{2\sigma^4}{n-1} = \frac{2(n-1)\sigma^4}{n^2}$$
The MSE of the biased estimator $\hat{\sigma}^2$ is the sum of its variance and the square of its bias:
$$MSE(\hat{\sigma}^2) = Var(\hat{\sigma}^2) + \text{Bias}^2(\hat{\sigma}^2) = \frac{2(n-1)\sigma^4}{n^2} + \left(-\frac{1}{n}\sigma^2\right)^2 = \frac{2n-2+1}{n^2}\sigma^4 = \frac{2n-1}{n^2}\sigma^4$$

To compare the two MSEs, we consider their ratio or subtract them. Let's compare the denominators equivalently.
We want to show $MSE(\hat{\sigma}^2) < MSE(S^2)$:
$$\frac{2n-1}{n^2} < \frac{2}{n-1}$$
$$(2n-1)(n-1) < 2n^2$$
$$2n^2 - 2n - n + 1 < 2n^2 \implies -3n + 1 < 0 \implies 3n > 1$$
Since $n \ge 2$ (for $S^2$ to be defined), $3n > 1$ is always true.
Therefore, the biased estimator $\hat{\sigma}^2$ has a strictly smaller MSE than the unbiased estimator $S^2$, exemplifying the bias-variance tradeoff where introducing a small bias reduces the variance substantially enough to improve the overall MSE.

\subsection{Problem 4.39: Maximum Likelihood for Exponential Distribution}
\textbf{Solution:}
\textbf{Part (a): Log-likelihood and ML estimate}
Given $f(y; \lambda) = \lambda e^{-\lambda y}$, the likelihood function for a sample of size $n$ is:
$$l(\lambda; y) = \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda e^{-\lambda y_i} = \lambda^n e^{-\lambda \sum_{i=1}^n y_i}$$
Taking the natural logarithm, we obtain the log-likelihood function:
$$L(\lambda) = \ln l(\lambda; y) = n \ln \lambda - \lambda \sum_{i=1}^n y_i$$
To find the Maximum Likelihood Estimate (MLE), we take the derivative with respect to $\lambda$ and set it to 0 (score function):
$$S(\lambda) = \frac{\partial L(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda} = \frac{n}{\lambda} - \sum_{i=1}^n y_i = 0$$
Solving for $\hat{\lambda}$:
$$\frac{n}{\hat{\lambda}} = \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \implies \hat{\lambda} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^n y_i} = \frac{1}{\bar{y}}$$
Thus, the ML estimate for $\lambda$ is $1/\bar{y}$.

\textbf{Part (b): Fisher Information and Standard Error}
The Fisher Information $I(\lambda)$ is the negative expected value of the second derivative of the log-likelihood function evaluated over one observation (or the total information $I_n(\lambda)$ for $n$ observations).
$$\frac{\partial^2 L(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda^2} = -\frac{n}{\lambda^2}$$
Since there is no random variable $Y$ in the second derivative, the expected value is strictly the expression itself:
$$I_n(\lambda) = E\left[-\frac{\partial^2 L(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda^2}\right] = \frac{n}{\lambda^2}$$
The asymptotic variance of the MLE $\hat{\lambda}$ is the inverse of the Fisher information:
$$Var(\hat{\lambda}) \approx \frac{1}{I_n(\lambda)} = \frac{\lambda^2}{n}$$
Thus, the approximate large-sample standard error of $\hat{\lambda}$ is:
$$SE(\hat{\lambda}) = \frac{\hat{\lambda}}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1}{\bar{y}\sqrt{n}}$$

\textbf{Part (c): Plotting the relative log-likelihood}
For $\bar{y} = 10$, we have $\hat{\lambda} = 1/10 = 0.1$.
Substitute into $L(\lambda)$:
$$L(\lambda) - L(\hat{\lambda}) = \left( n \ln \lambda - 10n\lambda \right) - \left( n \ln(0.1) - 10n(0.1) \right) = n \left[ \ln \left(\frac{\lambda}{0.1}\right) - 10(\lambda - 0.1) \right]$$

    \caption{Relative Log-Likelihood curve for exponential estimator across varying $n$}
\end{figure}

The vertical line marks the true estimate $\hat{\lambda}=0.1$. As $n$ increases, the log-likelihood function becomes dramatically sharper around the maximum, signifying precisely reduced uncertainty and a drastically lower standard error (the curve approximates a steep quadratic/parabola per the Fisher Information).

We cross-categorize Marijuana usage as the outcome/response and Alcohol usage as the group.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Group 1 (Alcohol = Yes): $n_1 = 1949$. Used Marijuana ($m_1) = 911 + 44 = 955$. Estimated probability: $p_1 = 955 / 1949 = 0.4900$.
    \item Group 2 (Alcohol = No): $n_2 = 327$. Used Marijuana ($m_2) = 3 + 2 = 5$. Estimated probability: $p_2 = 5 / 327 = 0.0153$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{(a) Formula (4.13) - Wald 95\% CI:}
The standard error of the difference in independent proportions is:
$$SE(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) = \sqrt{\frac{p_1(1-p_1)}{n_1} + \frac{p_2(1-p_2)}{n_2}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.49(0.51)}{1949} + \frac{0.015(0.985)}{327}} \approx 0.01320$$
For a 95\% confidence level, $z = 1.96$.
$$\text{Margin of Error} = 1.96 \times 0.01320 \approx 0.02587$$
$$\text{Point Estimate} = p_1 - p_2 = 0.4900 - 0.0153 = 0.4747$$
The 95\% Wald CI is:
$$CI = 0.4747 \pm 0.02587 = (0.4488, 0.5006)$$

\textbf{(b) Statistical Software (prop.test):}
Using `prop.test` in R, setting `correct=FALSE` to disable Yates continuity correction (which mirrors strict asymptotic Wald test bounds).
The output yields the Confidence Interval:
95 percent confidence interval:
$0.4488310, 0.5005777$

\textbf{Conclusion:} 
The formula exactly aligns with strictly uncorrected statistical software. \subsection{Problem 4.45: Confidence Interval under Multinomial Distribution}
\textbf{Solution:}
\textbf{Part (a)}:
Given probabilities $\pi_1 = \pi^2, \pi_2 = 2\pi(1-\pi), \pi_3 = (1-\pi)^2$ and sum of counts $n = y_1 + y_2 + y_3$.
The likelihood is:
$$l(\pi) \propto (\pi^2)^{y_1} (2\pi(1-\pi))^{y_2} ((1-\pi)^2)^{y_3}$$
Taking the natural logarithm yields the log-likelihood $L(\pi)$:
$$L(\pi) = c + y_1(2 \ln \pi) + y_2(\ln 2 + \ln \pi + \ln(1-\pi)) + y_3(2 \ln(1-\pi))$$
$$L(\pi) = c' + (2y_1 + y_2)\ln \pi + (y_2 + 2y_3)\ln(1-\pi)$$
To find the MLE, differentiate with respect to $\pi$ and set equal to $0$:
$$\frac{dL(\pi)}{d\pi} = \frac{2y_1 + y_2}{\pi} - \frac{y_2 + 2y_3}{1-\pi} = 0$$
$$(2y_1 + y_2)(1-\pi) = (y_2 + 2y_3)\pi \implies 2y_1 + y_2 - \pi(2y_1 + y_2) = \pi y_2 + 2\pi y_3$$
$$2y_1 + y_2 = \pi(2y_1 + y_2 + y_2 + 2y_3) = \pi(2y_1 + 2y_2 + 2y_3) = \pi(2n)$$
$$\hat{\pi} = \frac{2y_1 + y_2}{2n}$$

\textbf{Part (b)}:
Find Fisher Information $I(\pi)$:
First derive the second derivative:
$$\frac{d^2L(\pi)}{d\pi^2} = -\frac{2y_1 + y_2}{\pi^2} - \frac{y_2 + 2y_3}{(1-\pi)^2}$$
Substitute expectations into the derivative to retrieve Information matrix representation:
$E[y_1] = n\pi^2, \quad E[y_2] = n[2\pi(1-\pi)], \quad E[y_3] = n(1-\pi)^2$
$$E[2y_1 + y_2] = 2n\pi^2 + 2n\pi(1-\pi) = 2n\pi$$
$$E[y_2 + 2y_3] = 2n\pi(1-\pi) + 2n(1-\pi)^2 = 2n(1-\pi)$$
$$I_n(\pi) = -E\left[\frac{d^2L}{d\pi^2}\right] = \frac{2n\pi}{\pi^2} + \frac{2n(1-\pi)}{(1-\pi)^2} = \frac{2n}{\pi} + \frac{2n}{1-\pi} = \frac{2n}{\pi(1-\pi)}$$
Large-sample standard error of MLE:
$$SE(\hat{\pi}) \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{I_n(\hat{\pi})}} = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{\pi}(1-\hat{\pi})}{2n}}$$

\textbf{Part (c)}:
Assuming large enough $n$, MLE distributions normally approximate. Thus, a (Wald-type) 95\% confidence interval leverages $Z \approx 1.96$:
$$CI_{95\%} = \hat{\pi} \pm 1.96 \times SE(\hat{\pi}) = \hat{\pi} \pm 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{\hat{\pi}(1-\hat{\pi})}{2n}}$$

\subsection{Problem 4.64: Bias-Variance Tradeoff (Bayes vs ML Estimator)}
\textbf{Solution:}
\textbf{Part (a):}
For the Bayesian estimator: 
$E[\tilde{\pi}] = \frac{E[Y]+1}{n+2} = \frac{n\pi + 1}{n+2}$.
Thus, the bias is:
$$\text{Bias}(\tilde{\pi}) = E[\tilde{\pi}] - \pi = \frac{n\pi + 1}{n+2} - \pi = \frac{n\pi + 1 - n\pi - 2\pi}{n+2} = \frac{1 - 2\pi}{n+2}$$

$$Var(\tilde{\pi}) = Var\left(\frac{Y+1}{n+2}\right) = \frac{1}{(n+2)^2} Var(Y) = \frac{n\pi(1-\pi)}{(n+2)^2}$$

Comparison against ML $\hat{\pi} = Y/n$:
ML Estimator is structurally unbiased ($\text{Bias} = 0$), contrasting the non-zero bias inherently injected into the Bayesian estimator.
ML Estimator Variance is $\frac{\pi(1-\pi)}{n}$. The variance of the Bayes estimator is generally structurally smaller, especially mitigating extreme variability near zero bounds.

\textbf{Part (b): Mean Squared Errors}
$$MSE(\tilde{\pi}) = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Var} = \left(\frac{1-2\pi}{n+2}\right)^2 + \frac{n\pi(1-\pi)}{(n+2)^2} = \frac{n\pi(1-\pi) + (1-2\pi)^2}{(n+2)^2}$$
